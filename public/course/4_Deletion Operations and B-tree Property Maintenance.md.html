Key redistribution during deletion in B-trees is crucial for maintaining two essential properties: minimum occupancy and balance. When a key is deleted, the process ensures that nodes remain at least half-full (minimum occupancy) and that the tree remains balanced from root to leaves. This is achieved through a series of operations:

  1. Key Borrowing: If a node falls below minimum occupancy after deletion, it first attempts to borrow a key from a sibling node.
  2. Node Merging: If borrowing isn't possible, nodes are merged to maintain the minimum occupancy requirement.
  3. Root Adjustment: In case the root node has only one child after deletion, it's eliminated to preserve tree height.

The concept of minimum occupancy in B-tree nodes is intrinsically linked to the efficiency of deletion operations. By maintaining a lower bound on node occupancy (typically 50%), B-trees ensure:

  1. Bounded Complexity: Deletion operations have a guaranteed upper bound on the number of nodes affected, ensuring consistent performance.
  2. Space Efficiency: Minimum occupancy prevents underutilization of allocated memory, balancing storage costs with access speed.
  3. Predictable I/O: In disk-based implementations, minimum occupancy ensures a predictable number of disk accesses for operations.

While minimum occupancy is crucial, it's worth considering the trade-offs:

  1. Flexibility vs. Rigidity: A strict 50% minimum occupancy might lead to more frequent redistributions. Would a more flexible approach (e.g., 33% minimum) offer better performance in certain scenarios?
  2. Deletion-Insertion Balance: In write-heavy systems with frequent deletions and insertions, how might adjusting the minimum occupancy threshold affect overall performance? Could a dynamic threshold based on workload characteristics be beneficial?
  3. Cache Considerations: How does minimum occupancy interact with modern CPU cache architectures? Might looser occupancy constraints lead to better cache utilization in some cases?
